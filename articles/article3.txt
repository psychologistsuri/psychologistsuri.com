## Introduction

About one out of every 36 children in the United States has a diagnosis of Autism Spectrum Disorder (ASD). Autism is not a disease. It is a difference in how the brain grows and works. Many autistic kids find help through clear supports. These supports strengthen how they talk. They help manage feelings. They teach skills for daily life.

Every child on the spectrum is different. Some need a small amount of help. Others need a lot of support every day. The best approach does not try to change who the child is. Instead, it provides clear rules and systems. This lowers stress. As a result, new skills have a chance to grow.

In the United States, behavioral therapy has a specific goal. It helps children learn useful skills. It lowers distress. It builds independence. At the same time, it respects the child’s personality. It honors the family’s choices and the child's unique brain.

---

## What Is Behavioral Therapy?

Behavioral therapy is a group of proven methods. These methods teach skills by connecting an action to a result. The most common model in the United States is Applied Behavior Analysis. People often call this ABA. Schools also use methods based in the classroom or natural play.

Behavioral therapy usually targets these areas:

- Communication, listening, and using language  
- Playing with others and being social  
- Handling big emotions calmly  
- Taking care of daily needs like dressing  
- Stopping behaviors that are unsafe or disrupt others  
- Doing things alone and joining in  

Modern behavioral therapy focuses on:

- Giving rewards for good choices instead of punishing bad ones  
- Teaching a new skill instead of only stopping an action  
- Learning by watching others, practicing, and doing things in real life  

---

## How Behavioral Therapy Works

This therapy starts with a search for answers. We need to know why a behavior happens. Experts look for patterns. They watch carefully to see:

- What the behavior looks like  
- When and where it takes place  
- What happens right before the action  
- What happens right after the action  
- What the child gets or avoids by doing it  

Science tells us something important. Behavior is a form of communication.

**Examples:**

- Hitting might show the child feels overloaded.  
- Running away might be an attempt to escape a loud noise.  
- Screaming might be the only way to ask for help or show anger.  

Therapy teaches safer ways to ask for things. It teaches better ways to say what is wrong. Over time, the child learns to use words or signs. These new skills replace the unsafe actions.

## Common Types of Behavioral Therapy in the United States

### 1. Applied Behavior Analysis (ABA)

ABA uses clear teaching steps. It relies on practice. It uses rewards to build skills like:

- Communication  
- Getting ready for school work  
- Self-help habits  
- Managing emotions  

New ABA plans often happen during play. They happen during normal daily tasks. This is different from sitting at a table for drills. The best goals fit the child’s real life. They support freedom in the long run.

### 2. Positive Behavior Support (PBS)

Schools use PBS often. This method focuses on:

- Changing the room to lower stress and stop problems  
- Teaching kids how to cope and stay calm  
- Removing things that upset the child and keeping routines strong  
- Helping kids make friends and feel like they belong  

### 3. Naturalistic Developmental Behavioral Interventions (NDBI)

You will often see these models:

- Early Start Denver Model (ESDM)  
- Pivotal Response Treatment (PRT)  

These methods mix science with play. The child leads the way. Adults follow what the child likes. Then, the adult adds learning moments. This builds social skills and readiness to learn in a natural way.

## Behavioral Therapy in United States Public Schools

Public schools have teams to deliver support. You will find these helps through:

- IEPs (Individualized Education Programs)  
- BIPs (Behavior Intervention Plans)  
- School psychologists  
- Behavior experts  
- Special education teachers  
- Other service providers  

A law called the Individuals with Disabilities Education Act (IDEA) protects these rights. Schools must give the right support when autism changes how a student learns. These supports should remove blocks. They should lower sadness. They help students join school activities in a meaningful way.

---

## Examples of School-Based Program Models

School districts often have special programs for autistic students. The models differ from place to place. Most combine school work with social and behavioral help.

### Examples from publicly described programs

#### New York City

- ASD Nest  
- ASD Horizon  
- AIMS  

These programs mix math and reading with help for talking and making friends. Services happen inside public schools. They fit the specific needs of different students.

#### Arlington Public Schools, Virginia

- Mini-MIPA  
- MIPA  

These classrooms use clear routines. They use step-by-step teaching. They use pictures to help kids talk. This builds attention. It helps kids take part in class. Small groups are often part of the plan.

## Ethical Standards and Child-Centered Practice

Good therapy follows strict rules. It puts the child first. This includes:

- Respecting the child’s dignity, choices, and freedom  
- Staying away from force or harmful actions  
- Focusing on goals that matter for real life  
- Asking families for permission and help  
- Respecting culture and age  

Good support puts safety first. It puts happiness first. It is never about control.

---

## How Behavioral Therapy Helps

When experts use therapy in an ethical way, it works well. It helps children:

- Express wants and needs better  
- Feel less worry and anger  
- Play and mix with others  
- Do things on their own  
- Join in school and daily tasks  

You will see the best results when you use these tools everywhere. The plan should be the same at home. It should be the same at school. Skills grow faster this way.

## Tools for Autism Support in United States Public Schools

### Behavior support
- **Area of Support:** Behavior support  
- **Tool or Service:** FBA (Functional Behavior Assessment)  
- **What It Does:** Finds out what triggers an action and what the action is trying to say  
- **How Schools Use It:** Used to create supports and pick the right goals  

- **Area of Support:** Behavior support  
- **Tool or Service:** BIP (Behavior Intervention Plan)  
- **What It Does:** A written plan to prevent problems, teach skills, and guide adult actions  
- **How Schools Use It:** Teachers use this in every classroom and school area  

### Communication
- **Area of Support:** Communication  
- **Tool or Service:** Speech therapy and AAC supports  
- **What It Does:** Builds language skills, understanding, and clear ways to talk  
- **How Schools Use It:** Added to IEP services and used during lessons  

### Structure and routines
- **Area of Support:** Structure and routines  
- **Tool or Service:** Visual schedules and task charts  
- **What It Does:** Shows what happens next and supports freedom  
- **How Schools Use It:** Used every day in classrooms to help transitions  

### Social interaction
- **Area of Support:** Social interaction  
- **Tool or Service:** Social skills lessons and peer supports  
- **What It Does:** Teaches turn-taking, how to talk, and how to join a group  
- **How Schools Use It:** Practiced in real activities with help from adults  

### Regulation and sensory support
- **Area of Support:** Regulation and sensory support  
- **Tool or Service:** Break plans, calm spaces, movement supports  
- **What It Does:** Stops feelings of overload and helps the student stay calm  
- **How Schools Use It:** Used early before stress gets too high  

---

## Conclusion

Autism is not something to fix. But support makes daily life easier. It makes success more likely.

Behavioral therapy should be kind. It should fit the child. It should use proven methods. When this happens, children build skills for talking and coping. They gain freedom. This helps them take part in school. It helps them join the community. Success often shows up in small ways. Perhaps a child asks for help. Perhaps they handle a change in plans. Perhaps they join friends during a game.

The goal is not to make a child different. The goal is to help a child thrive.
