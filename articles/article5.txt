# Parenting Children With Disabilities: Part 2  
## Part 2: Helping Your Child With Emotions and Behavior  
**Surendra Bisht**  
Educational Psychologist and Inclusive Education Specialist  

## Why Big Feelings Are Common  
Lots of kids with disabilities feel things deeply. It is intense. Their nervous systems spark fast. Then it takes a long time for them to cool off. A tiny problem feels huge. A shift in the daily plan feels scary.  

You think this is bad behavior. You assume it shows a lack of discipline. That is not true. It is simply how their brain handles stress.  

## What Behavior Is Communicating  
Behavior acts as a language. It is often the only way a child knows how to speak to you. They use their actions to tell you:  

- I feel tired  
- I feel scared  
- I do not understand what you want  
- This feels like too much right now  
- I need your help  

Think about when kids hit or cry. Or when they refuse to move. Or when they shut down completely. These actions show that something inside feels wrong. It is not working right in that specific moment.  

Punishment might stop the yelling for a minute. But it fails to fix the root issue. The problem stays there.  

## Teach Emotional Skills During Calm Moments  
Kids do not learn well during a meltdown. Their brains are offline. The best time to teach is when they feel calm and happy. You have many tools to try:  

- Naming the feelings out loud  
- Practicing deep breathing together  
- Using charts with faces on them  
- Teaching specific words for frustration and fear  
- Acting out hard situations before they happen  

These steps help build awareness. They give your child skills to cope.  

## Supporting Self-Regulation  
Some kids need to move their bodies first. They must settle their physical self before they settle their mind. Movement acts as a reset button. Examples include:  

- Walking around the room  
- Stretching their arms and legs  
- Jumping up and down  
- Squeezing a soft stress ball  
- Listening to their favorite music  
- Deep pressure on their arms  

Every single kid is different. You must watch closely. Notice what brings your child back to a balanced state.  

## Avoiding Power Struggles  
Fighting for control adds stress. It hurts you. It hurts your child. A child who feels out of control is unable to use logic. Reasoning wonâ€™t work. Try these ideas when you are able:  

- Offer two simple choices  
- Use short and clear words  
- Reduce what you ask for during emotional overload  
- Focus on keeping everyone safe first  

Connection works better than control. It builds trust.  

## What Real Progress Looks Like  
Making progress takes time. It happens slowly. Sometimes it feels uneven. You rarely see big dramatic changes all at once. Look for the small wins. You ought to celebrate:  

- Meltdowns that are shorter than before  
- Recovering faster after a hard moment  
- Asking you for help  
- Using one single calming strategy  

These are huge steps. They mean you are growing.  

## Final Thoughts  
Your child does not need you to take their feelings away. That is impossible. Instead, they need you to help them survive the wave of emotion. They need help understanding it. You stay calm. You stay supportive. This makes you their emotional anchor. You are the steady rock they rely on. This support builds strength over time. It creates resilience for the future.
